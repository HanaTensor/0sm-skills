0-Sphere Model — Explainer Note

Why the Compton Wavelength Cancels
— and What It Tells Us About Gravity

Satoshi Hanamura (2026) · Supplementary Explainer to Paper #52
Intended for graduate students (Master's and PhD) and general readers with an interest in physics

Galileo showed that a cannonball and a feather — in the absence of air resistance — fall at exactly the same rate. Newton accepted this as an empirical fact. Einstein elevated it to a foundational axiom of general relativity: the weak equivalence principle. But neither of them explained why it is true. The 0-Sphere Model proposes a structural answer through what we call the Compton wavelength cancellation theorem.

This note walks through the argument step by step, using only ideas from classical mechanics and high-school-level physics. No prior knowledge of quantum field theory is required.

1. What Is the Puzzle, Exactly?

In Newtonian mechanics there are actually two kinds of mass, and they are conceptually different:

F = mi · a     (inertial mass: "resistance to being pushed")
F = mg · g     (gravitational mass: "coupling strength to gravity")

Inertial mass measures how hard it is to accelerate an object. Gravitational mass measures how strongly gravity pulls on it. There is no logical reason why these two should be equal — they are defined by completely different operations. Yet experiment confirms mi = mg to better than one part in ten trillion.

The deep question

Why does the "resistance to being pushed" equal the "strength of gravitational coupling" to such extraordinary precision? Is this a coincidence, or is there a structural reason?

Einstein's answer was to declare it a postulate — a foundational assumption, not a derived result. The 0-Sphere Model attempts to go one level deeper and derive it.

2. The Electron's Internal Structure: Starting Point of the 0-Sphere Model

The 0-Sphere Model proposes that the electron has an internal structure: a photon sphere that oscillates back and forth between two points — Kernel A and Kernel B — separated by approximately the Compton wavelength λC = ℏ/(mec). This rapid oscillation is called Zitterbewegung (ZB), German for "trembling motion."

High-school analogy

Think of a mass on a spring undergoing simple harmonic motion. The electron's "spring" is an internal radiation gradient F(θ) = E0 sin θ. The two turning points of the oscillation are Kernel A and Kernel B.

Two quantities are central to the argument:

1

Energy collected per cycle (Role I: the lever arm)

In a gravitational field, Kernel A and Kernel B are at slightly different heights. The difference in gravitational potential energy between the two turning points — the energy "collected" by the electron per ZB oscillation — depends on how far apart they are. That separation is set by λC.

2

Cycle rate (Role II: the clock)

How many times per second does the electron oscillate between Kernel A and Kernel B? This rate is the ZB frequency ωZB = vZB/λC. Again, λC appears — this time in the denominator: a longer oscillation path means a slower clock.

Notice that λC appears in both roles, but in opposite ways: larger λC means more energy collected per cycle (Role I), but also a slower cycle rate (Role II). The two effects pull in opposite directions.

3. The Calculation: How the Force Emerges

In a gravitational field g, the ZB axis vector ê makes some angle with the gravitational direction ĝ. The asymmetry in turning-point energies is:

δE = me g λC (ê · ĝ)

The factor λC appears in the numerator — this is the lever arm of Role I. Meanwhile, the cycle rate is:

ωZB / 2π = vZB / λC

The factor λC appears in the denominator — this is the clock of Role II. The gravitational force is the net momentum transferred per cycle multiplied by the cycle rate:

F  =  δE/c  ×  ωZB/2π  =  (me g λC / c)  ×  (vZB / λC)  =  me g βZB

λC cancels exactly between numerator and denominator. What remains is the dimensionless ratio βZB = vZB/c ≈ 0.04, which is determined entirely by the electron's anomalous magnetic moment — no free parameters.

Why this matters

The Compton wavelength depends on mass: λC = ℏ/(mc). An electron and a muon (about 207 times heavier) have very different values of λC. But since λC cancels, the gravitational acceleration F/m = g βZB is the same for both. All particles fall at the same rate — this is the weak equivalence principle, derived rather than postulated.

4. Why the Cancellation Is Structural, Not Accidental

It is important to understand that the cancellation of λC is not a lucky algebraic coincidence. There is a geometric reason why it must cancel, and it comes down to a single key observation:

The key insight

The spatial scale that determines how much gravitational energy is collected per cycle (Role I: lever arm ∝ λC) and the time scale that determines how fast that collection happens (Role II: cycle rate ∝ 1/λC) are both governed by the same geometric object: the ZB axis vector ê.

Because both appearances of λC share a common geometric origin in ê, they cancel in the ratio. The cancellation is a structural consequence of this shared origin, not an accident.

The string wave speed analogy

A precise and illuminating analogy is the speed of a wave on a stretched string:

v = √(T / μ)

Here T is the tension and μ is the linear mass density. Both vary widely across different strings — a thin steel wire versus a thick rubber band, say. Yet the formula always takes the same universal form: tension over density, under a square root.

The string

T and μ can each take any value independently on different strings. Yet their ratio T/μ always yields the wave speed in the same universal form.

Why? Because both T and μ are properties of the same physical object — the string. Their shared origin structures the relationship between them.

The 0-Sphere electron

λC appears in the energy numerator and in the cycle-rate denominator. Yet it cancels exactly, leaving F = meg βZB in a universal form.

Why? Because both appearances of λC are governed by the same geometric object — the ZB axis ê. Their shared origin makes the cancellation inevitable.

In the string case, the individual values of T and μ do not cancel — they combine to give the wave speed. In the 0-Sphere case, the individual appearances of λC do cancel, because it appears symmetrically in numerator and denominator. But the underlying logic is the same: shared physical origin → structured relationship → universal result.

7. Summary

What this theory says

The weak equivalence principle — the fact that all particles fall at the same gravitational acceleration regardless of their mass — is not a coincidence and not merely a postulate. It is a geometric identity arising from the internal structure of matter.

Within the 0-Sphere Model, the Compton wavelength λC sets both the spatial scale of gravitational energy collection (Role I) and the temporal scale of oscillation (Role II), because both are governed by the same geometric object — the ZB axis vector ê. This shared origin forces λC to cancel from the force formula, making the gravitational acceleration independent of mass. The universality of free fall is encoded in the geometry of the electron's internal dynamics, not added as a separate assumption.
